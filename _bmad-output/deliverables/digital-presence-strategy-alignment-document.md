## 3. What We Identified

During and after our conversation, I identified several critical areas that will shape how this project succeeds. These are the questions and considerations that, if left unaddressed, would compromise the quality of anything we build.

### 3.1 Audience: Who Is This Website For?

A website that tries to speak to everyone speaks to no one. [COMPANY NAME]'s website will be visited by fundamentally different people with fundamentally different goals:

| Audience | What They Want | What They Need to See |
|----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Investors / Partners** | Scale, credibility, leadership, financial stability | Corporate overview, leadership team, track record, portfolio breadth |
| **Customers (Agriculture)** | Products, pricing, reliability, delivery | Product catalogues, farm operations, supply chain information |
| **Customers (Real Estate)** | Properties, availability, trustworthiness | Listings, project photos, location details, contact |
| **Customers (Oil & Gas)** | Capabilities, contract history, certifications | Service offerings, equipment, project portfolio |
| **Government / Regulatory** | Compliance, certifications, legitimacy | Licences, regulatory certifications (NAFDAC, NUPRC), corporate governance |
| **Job Seekers** | Culture, openings, growth opportunities | Career page, team culture, open positions |
| **General Public** | Understanding what the company does | Clear, simple overview with compelling visuals |

**The implication:** The homepage cannot be optimised for all of these simultaneously. We need to decide — together — who the primary audience is, and design the entry experience accordingly. This is a strategic business decision, not a design decision.

### 3.2 Scope: One Website or Seven?

This may be the single most important question of the entire project.

Does [COMPANY NAME] operate under one unified brand? Or do the divisions — agriculture, real estate, oil & gas — have separate identities in the market?

The answer determines whether we are building:

- **Option A:** One corporate group website with divisional sections (like Dangote Group)
- **Option B:** A family of related websites with a shared identity system (like Tata Group)
- **Option C:** A corporate parent site plus select standalone sites for divisions that need them

Each option has different implications for design complexity, development time, content requirements, and investment. We will resolve this together during the Discovery phase.

### 3.3 Content: The Silent Project Risk

Based on my experience with projects of this scale, the most common reason websites stall is not design disagreements or technical challenges — it is content.

Seven divisions could require:

- 50–100 pages of written content (descriptions, service pages, team profiles, case studies)
- Professional photography across all operations (farms, processing facilities, properties, petrol stations, offices)
- Team biographies and leadership profiles
- Certifications, licences, and compliance documentation
- Success stories and project portfolios for each division

**The question we must answer early:** How much of this content exists today? Who will create what doesn't exist? This directly impacts timeline and investment, and must be addressed before design begins.

### 3.4 The "Test of Time" Question

You mentioned wanting a website that stands the test of time — something you wouldn't need to revisit constantly. I respect that aspiration, and I want to be transparent about what it actually requires.

A website is a living asset. Markets change, team members change, new projects are completed, new verticals may be added. A website that never changes becomes a website that looks abandoned — and in the eyes of visitors, an abandoned website signals an inactive business.

**What "stands the test of time" actually means in practice:**

| What Doesn't Change | What Must Change |
|---------------------|------------------|
| Brand identity and visual system | News, updates, and announcements |
| Core site architecture and navigation | Team members and leadership |
| Design system and component library | Project portfolios and case studies |
| Technology foundation | Product/service offerings as they evolve |
| Security and performance infrastructure | Regulatory and compliance information |

The solution is not a website that never needs updating. The solution is a website built on a foundation so solid that updates are easy, fast, and don't require a developer. That's what we'll build.

## 5. Understanding Brand Architecture

A conglomerate with seven divisions faces a strategic question that most single-industry businesses never encounter: **How do the divisions relate to each other visually?**

There are four established models:

### Model A: Branded House
*One master brand, all divisions underneath*

**Example:** Google (Google Maps, Google Drive, Google Cloud — all Google)

**What it looks like for [COMPANY NAME]:** Every division carries the [COMPANY NAME] name and visual identity. "[COMPANY NAME] Agriculture," "[COMPANY NAME] Real Estate," "[COMPANY NAME] Energy."

**Advantage:** Maximum brand cohesion. Every division reinforces the parent.
**Consideration:** Divisions feel less independent. May not suit if divisions serve very different markets.

### Model B: House of Brands
*Each division has its own independent identity*

**Example:** Unilever (Dove, Axe, Lipton — no visible connection to Unilever)

**What it looks like for [COMPANY NAME]:** Each division has its own name, logo, and visual identity. The parent company exists only at the corporate level.

**Advantage:** Each division can speak directly to its specific market.
**Consideration:** Expensive to develop and maintain. Loses the "conglomerate strength" narrative.

### Model C: Endorsed Brands
*Divisions have their own identity, backed by the parent*

**Example:** Marriott (Courtyard by Marriott, Ritz-Carlton — A Marriott International Brand)

**What it looks like for [COMPANY NAME]:** Each division has its own personality but carries the [COMPANY NAME] endorsement. "Powered by [COMPANY NAME]" or "A [COMPANY NAME] Company."

**Advantage:** Best of both — division independence with parent credibility.
**Consideration:** Requires careful balance in visual hierarchy.

### Model D: Hybrid
*Different approaches for different divisions based on strategic need*

**What it looks like for [COMPANY NAME]:** Some divisions (e.g., Commodity Marketing, Import/Export) operate under the parent brand, while others (e.g., Real Estate, Oil & Gas) have more independent identities because they serve very different markets.

**Advantage:** Maximum flexibility.
**Consideration:** Requires the most strategic thought and the most comprehensive brand guidelines.

---

**We will determine the right model for [COMPANY NAME] together during the Discovery phase.** This is not a design decision — it is a business strategy decision that affects how your company is perceived in every market you operate in.

## 9. A Preview of What's Possible

To give you a sense of the creative direction, here are two concepts we would explore during the design phase:

### Concept 1: Story-First Homepage
*"From the Soil to the Shelf to the World"*

Instead of presenting seven divisions as separate boxes on a homepage, we tell the story of [COMPANY NAME] as an integrated journey:

**The Narrative Flow:**
The land produces → We farm and raise livestock → We process the harvest → We market the commodities → We build the infrastructure → We trade globally → We power the economy

Each scroll section introduces a division as a chapter in a larger story. The visitor doesn't see seven unrelated businesses — they see one powerful conglomerate operating across the entire value chain.

**Why this works:** It turns your complexity from a navigation problem into your greatest selling point.

### Concept 2: Audience-Guided Entry
*"I am an..."*

Instead of forcing every visitor through the same homepage funnel, we greet them with a simple question:

**"Welcome to [COMPANY NAME]. I am..."**

- An **Investor** looking to understand the portfolio → routed to corporate overview, financials, leadership
- A **Customer** looking for products or services → routed to relevant divisional pages
- A **Partner** exploring collaboration → routed to capabilities, project history, contact
- A **Job Seeker** looking for opportunities → routed to careers, culture, openings

Each path curates content from across all seven divisions, tailored to that visitor's specific needs.

**Why this works:** A website that understands its visitor before they even scroll feels intelligent, considered, and professional.

## 14. Why a Structured Approach Matters

I understand the desire to move quickly. Speed is valuable. But in projects of this scope, speed without strategy produces expensive problems:

| When Projects Skip Discovery | What Typically Happens |
|-----------------------------|----------------------|
| No audience analysis | Homepage designed for the wrong people. Redesign required. |
| No brand guidelines | Inconsistent visual identity across the site. Every page feels disconnected. |
| No content strategy | Design is approved. Development is complete. Launch stalls because no one wrote the content. |
| No information architecture | Navigation confuses visitors. Key pages are buried. Bounce rates are high. |
| No approval workflow | Conflicting feedback from multiple stakeholders. Endless revision cycles. 8-week projects become 20-week projects. |
| No maintenance plan | Site launches well. Six months later, it shows outdated information. A year later, it's a liability. |

**A structured approach does not slow the project down. It prevents the project from being done twice.**

The fastest path to a world-class result is doing it right the first time.
