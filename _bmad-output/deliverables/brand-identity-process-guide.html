Section 01

What Brand Identity Actually Is (And Is Not)

Before you can audit, create, or digitise a brand, you need to be completely clear on what a brand identity is. This matters because clients frequently confuse a brand with a logo, or with marketing, or with advertising. When you can explain the difference clearly, you immediately position yourself as someone who understands the bigger picture. That builds trust and justifies higher fees.

The Brand vs. the Brand Identity

Think of it this way. The brand is the perception that lives inside people's heads. It is the total sum of how customers feel when they think about a business. You cannot design a brand directly, the same way you cannot directly design someone's opinion of you. What you can design is the brand identity: the intentional collection of visual, verbal, and experiential elements that shape that perception over time.

A brand identity is your client's best tool for influencing how people think and feel about their business. It is not the final destination. It is the vehicle.

The Components of a Brand Identity

A complete brand identity has three layers:

1. Brand Strategy (The Foundation)

This is the thinking that happens before any design work. It answers fundamental questions:

  • Mission — Why does this business exist beyond making money?
  • Vision — What does the business want to become in 5 to 10 years?
  • Values — What principles guide decisions and behaviour?
  • Positioning — What unique space does this business occupy in the market?
  • Target audience — Who specifically is this business trying to reach?
  • Brand personality — If this brand were a person, what would they be like?

2. Visual Identity (What People See)

  • Logo and logo variations (primary, secondary, icon/mark, single-colour)
  • Colour palette (primary, secondary, accent, neutral colours with codes for every format)
  • Typography (heading font, body font, UI font, size scale, weight usage)
  • Photography and imagery style (subject matter, treatment, mood, filters)
  • Iconography (style of icons, line weight, corner treatment)
  • Layout and composition principles (grid, spacing, visual hierarchy approach)
  • Graphic elements (patterns, textures, shapes, dividers, backgrounds)

3. Verbal Identity (What People Read and Hear)

  • Tone of voice (formal vs casual, playful vs serious, technical vs simple)
  • Key messages (tagline, value propositions, elevator pitch)
  • Vocabulary (words the brand uses and avoids)
  • Writing style (sentence length, paragraph structure, use of jargon)

What Brand Identity Is NOT

Common Misconception

"Brand identity is just a logo."

A logo is one component of a visual identity. Designing only a logo leaves the client with no system, no consistency, and no guidelines for how everything else should look and sound.

Reality

A logo is a single element within a much larger system.

The identity includes every visual and verbal touchpoint. A strong brand identity means that even without the logo visible, people recognise the brand from its colours, typography, photography style, and tone of voice.

Common Misconception

"Branding and marketing are the same thing."

Marketing is the set of activities you use to promote a business. Branding is the identity system that makes those activities recognisable and consistent.

Reality

Brand identity is the foundation that marketing is built on top of.

Without a brand identity, each piece of marketing looks like it comes from a different company. The brand identity is what makes marketing accumulate recognition over time rather than starting from scratch every campaign.

Common Misconception

"Branding is just about looking pretty."

This treats brand identity as decoration, something nice to have but not essential.

Reality

Brand identity is a strategic business tool.

It communicates credibility, professionalism, and differentiation. A well-designed brand identity makes a business look established and trustworthy, which directly affects whether customers choose them over competitors.

Common Misconception

"Advertising is branding."

Advertising is a paid channel for distributing messages. It is one of many activities that express the brand, but it is not the brand itself.

Reality

Advertising is one expression of the brand identity, not the identity itself.

The brand identity tells the advertising team what colours to use, what fonts to set headlines in, what tone of voice to write copy in, and what kinds of images to select. The identity is the source; advertising is one outlet.

Why This Matters for You as a Web Designer

When clients come to you for a website, they often have a fragmented or non-existent brand identity. If you build a website without first establishing (or auditing) the brand identity, you end up making dozens of visual and verbal decisions on the fly: picking colours that "seem right," choosing fonts that "look nice," writing button text in whatever tone comes to mind. The result is a website that works in isolation but does not connect to anything else the business does.

By offering brand identity services alongside web design, you can:

  • Solve a real problem your clients have (and most do not even know they have it)
  • Make your web design work faster, because design decisions flow from the brand system instead of being debated one at a time
  • Deliver a more valuable project, because the client gets a foundation they can use for years, not just a single website
  • Charge more, because you are solving a higher-level problem
  • Differentiate yourself from the thousands of web designers who only build pages
Key Insight

You do not need to become a "branding agency" to offer brand identity services. You need to understand the process, follow it rigorously, and deliver professional-quality work. The process in this guide gives you exactly that structure.

Section 02

Brand Audit Checklist

A brand audit is the first thing you do with any client, whether they are a new business that thinks they have "nothing" (they always have something) or an established company with years of accumulated materials. The audit tells you where things stand so you can make informed recommendations about what to keep, what to fix, and what to create from scratch.

For each item below, you will find four things: what to look for, where to find it, what "good" looks like versus what "needs work" looks like, and digital readiness criteria. Use this as a literal checklist. Print it out or copy it into a spreadsheet and go through every item with (or on behalf of) the client.

2.1 Logo Files

What to Look For
  • The primary logo (full colour, standard orientation)
  • Logo variations: horizontal/landscape, stacked/vertical, icon-only, wordmark-only
  • Single-colour versions (all black, all white, all navy, or whatever the primary brand colour is)
  • File formats available: vector (SVG, AI, EPS), raster (PNG with transparency), PDF
  • Size versions: large for print, medium for web, small for favicons
  • Clear space rules documented or at least understood
  • Minimum size specified
Where to Find It
  • Ask the client directly: "Can you send me every logo file you have?"
  • Check their Google Drive, Dropbox, or shared folders
  • Look at their current website (right-click, inspect, find the logo file)
  • Check their social media profile pictures and cover photos
  • Look at business cards, letterheads, invoices
  • Check email signatures
  • Ask who originally designed the logo and whether they can contact that designer for source files
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Vector source file (AI, SVG, or EPS) exists
  • Multiple variations available
  • PNG exports with transparent backgrounds
  • Logo is clean, scales well, and is legible at small sizes
  • Colour versions and mono versions exist
Needs Work
  • Only a JPEG exists (no transparency, loses quality)
  • Only one version, often embedded in a Word document or PowerPoint
  • Pixelated or blurry at larger sizes
  • Contains gradients or effects that do not scale
  • No vector source file; the original designer cannot be reached
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • SVG version exists and is optimised (under 20KB, clean paths, no embedded raster images)
  • PNG version at minimum 1024px wide with transparent background
  • Favicon version: a simplified icon that reads clearly at 32x32 pixels
  • Social media avatar version: works in a circle crop at 400x400 pixels
  • White (reversed) version for dark backgrounds
  • Open Graph version: logo placed on branded background at 1200x630 pixels

2.2 Colour Palette

What to Look For
  • Primary brand colours (usually 1 to 3 colours)
  • Secondary colours (supporting colours, usually 2 to 4)
  • Accent colour (a standout colour for calls to action and highlights)
  • Neutral colours (backgrounds, text, borders)
  • Whether colours are documented with exact values or just "whatever looks right"
  • Consistency of colours across materials (does the blue on the website match the blue on the business card?)
Where to Find It
  • Existing brand guidelines document (if one exists)
  • The current website's CSS (inspect element, look for colour values)
  • Logo files (sample colours from the logo using a colour picker)
  • Printed materials (scan or photograph, then sample colours, keeping in mind print and screen differ)
  • Social media templates or graphics
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Colours are documented with hex, RGB, and CMYK values
  • A clear hierarchy: primary, secondary, accent, neutral
  • Colours have sufficient contrast for accessibility (WCAG AA at minimum)
  • Consistent use across all touchpoints
  • The palette is intentional and supports the brand personality
Needs Work
  • Colours vary from one material to the next
  • No documented colour values; people eyeball it each time
  • Too many colours with no hierarchy
  • Poor contrast ratios (light grey text on white, for example)
  • Colours chosen without considering accessibility or digital use
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Every colour has a hex code documented
  • Every colour has RGB values documented
  • Every colour has HSL values documented (useful for programmatic adjustments)
  • CSS custom properties (variables) are defined for the palette
  • Contrast ratios between text and background colours meet WCAG AA (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text)
  • Tints and shades of each colour are defined for hover states, disabled states, and backgrounds
  • Dark mode variants are considered (or at minimum, colours work on both light and dark backgrounds)

2.3 Typography

What to Look For
  • Primary heading typeface
  • Body text typeface
  • UI/interface typeface (for buttons, labels, navigation) if different from body
  • Font weights in use (regular, medium, bold, etc.)
  • Whether the fonts are licensed for web use
  • Whether a type scale exists (defined sizes for H1 through H6, body, small text)
  • Line height and letter spacing preferences
Where to Find It
  • Existing brand guidelines
  • Current website CSS (inspect element, check font-family declarations)
  • Printed materials (use a tool like WhatTheFont or Identifont to identify typefaces)
  • Logo files (the logotype may use a specific font or a custom lettering)
  • Ask the client or previous designer what fonts were used
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • 2 to 3 typefaces maximum, with clear roles (heading, body, accent)
  • Fonts are web-compatible (available on Google Fonts, Adobe Fonts, or properly self-hosted)
  • A defined type scale with consistent sizing
  • Good readability at body text sizes
  • Proper licensing for web, print, and application use
Needs Work
  • 5+ random fonts used across different materials
  • Display fonts used for body text (poor readability)
  • Fonts that are not available for web use (print-only licences)
  • No consistent sizing or hierarchy
  • Fonts from dubious free download sites (licence unclear)
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Web font files exist in WOFF2 format (primary) and WOFF (fallback)
  • Google Fonts or Adobe Fonts availability confirmed
  • Font licence explicitly allows web embedding
  • Fallback font stack defined for each typeface
  • Type scale is defined using a modular scale (such as 1.25 ratio) or specific pixel/rem values
  • Line heights are defined (typically 1.4 to 1.7 for body text)
  • Font loading strategy is planned (font-display: swap, preloading)

2.4 Visual Language

What to Look For
  • Photography style: Are photos shot with consistent lighting, colour grading, composition?
  • Illustration style: If illustrations are used, is there a consistent style?
  • Graphic elements: Patterns, shapes, dividers, decorative elements
  • Icon style: Outlined vs filled, rounded vs sharp corners, consistent weight
  • Use of whitespace and layout density
  • Overall visual mood: minimalist, bold, warm, corporate, playful
Where to Find It
  • Current website (screenshot every page)
  • Social media posts (scroll through the last 3 to 6 months of posts)
  • Marketing materials: flyers, brochures, presentations
  • Physical environment: office signage, vehicle wraps, uniforms
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Photography follows a consistent treatment (same warmth, contrast, colour palette)
  • Graphics and icons share a unified style
  • There is a recognisable "look" even without the logo
  • Visual choices support the brand personality
Needs Work
  • Random stock photos with no consistent style
  • Mixed icon sets (some outlined, some filled, different weights)
  • Every piece of marketing looks like it came from a different company
  • No intentional graphic elements or patterns
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Photography style guide exists (or can be created) with examples of approved styles
  • Icon set is selected and available in SVG format
  • Graphic elements are available as scalable vectors
  • Image compression and optimisation guidelines are defined
  • Responsive image strategy is planned (srcset, art direction for different breakpoints)

2.5 Tone of Voice

What to Look For
  • How the brand "speaks" in writing: formal or casual? Technical or simple? Warm or authoritative?
  • Consistency across touchpoints: Does the website copy sound the same as social media posts?
  • Whether a tone of voice is documented or just happens organically
  • Use of jargon, slang, or industry terms
  • Sentence structure and length patterns
Where to Find It
  • Website copy (especially the "About" page, homepage, and product/service descriptions)
  • Social media captions and replies
  • Email marketing (newsletters, promotional emails)
  • Customer-facing documents (proposals, invoices, confirmation emails)
  • Internal communications (if accessible)
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Consistent tone across all platforms
  • Voice matches the target audience's expectations
  • A documented tone of voice guide exists
  • Writing is clear, engaging, and purposeful
Needs Work
  • Website sounds corporate, social media sounds like a teenager
  • No consistency between different writers or departments
  • Copy is generic and could belong to any company in the industry
  • Over-reliance on jargon or buzzwords
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Tone of voice principles are documented (at least 3 to 4 key traits)
  • Examples are provided for each trait showing "this, not that"
  • Guidelines exist for specific digital contexts: social media, email subject lines, error messages, button text, chatbot responses
  • A word list exists of terms the brand uses and terms it avoids

2.6 Brand Guidelines Document

What to Look For
  • Does a brand guidelines document exist at all?
  • How comprehensive is it? (One page? Fifty pages?)
  • When was it last updated?
  • Who has access to it?
  • Is it actually being followed?
Where to Find It
  • Ask the client directly
  • Check shared drives and folders labelled "Brand," "Marketing," or "Design"
  • Ask the marketing or design team (if one exists)
  • Check if the original logo designer provided any usage guidelines
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • A structured document covering logo, colour, typography, imagery, tone of voice
  • Updated within the last 2 years
  • Easily accessible to everyone who creates materials
  • Includes do's and don'ts with visual examples
  • Includes digital specifications (hex codes, font files, social media sizes)
Needs Work
  • No document exists
  • A one-page "style sheet" from 2015 that no one follows
  • Only covers the logo and colours (no typography, imagery, or voice)
  • Locked in a PDF that no one can find
  • Only has print specifications, no digital
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Guidelines document is available digitally (PDF or, better, an interactive web page)
  • Document includes all hex codes, RGB values, and font specifications for digital use
  • Document includes download links or references for all asset files
  • Document is version-controlled and dated

2.7 Business Cards and Stationery

What to Look For
  • Business card design: professional quality, consistent with other brand materials
  • Letterhead: printed or digital template
  • Envelopes, compliment slips, invoice templates
  • Presentation templates (PowerPoint, Google Slides, Keynote)
  • Document templates (proposals, reports, contracts)
Where to Find It
  • Ask the client for physical samples
  • Check their digital files for templates
  • Ask who designed them and whether source files exist
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • All stationery follows the same visual system
  • Editable templates exist that staff can use
  • Print quality is high
  • Contact information is accurate and current
Needs Work
  • Business cards look nothing like the website
  • Stationery was designed piecemeal by different people
  • No editable templates; staff create their own from scratch
  • Old phone numbers, outdated logos, inconsistent layouts
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Editable digital templates exist for all stationery items
  • Templates are in a format the client can use (Google Docs, Canva, Microsoft Office)
  • Digital letterhead is optimised for email and on-screen use (not just print)
  • Digital business card or vCard file exists for sharing via phone/email

2.8 Social Media Presence

What to Look For
  • Which platforms the client is active on
  • Profile picture: does it use the logo correctly?
  • Cover/header images: are they branded and current?
  • Bio text: is it clear, compelling, and consistent across platforms?
  • Post visuals: do they follow a consistent style?
  • Highlights/pinned content: is it curated and branded?
  • Link in bio: does it lead somewhere useful?
Where to Find It
  • Search for the business on every major platform: Instagram, Facebook, X (Twitter), LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok
  • Check if there are old or duplicate accounts
  • Ask the client for a complete list of their social media accounts
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Consistent profile images across all platforms
  • Branded cover images that are current
  • A clear, compelling bio with relevant links
  • Posts follow a visual style that is recognisable as the brand
  • Content templates are being used for consistency
Needs Work
  • Different profile pictures on different platforms
  • Cover images are outdated, stretched, or missing
  • Bio is vague, different on every platform, or missing key information
  • Posts use random colours, fonts, and layouts
  • Some platforms are abandoned (no posts in months)
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Profile picture: 400x400px PNG or JPG, works when cropped to a circle
  • Cover images sized correctly for each active platform
  • Social media content templates exist (at least 3 to 5 templates for different post types)
  • Templates are in a tool the client or their team can use (Canva is ideal for most small businesses)
  • Branded highlight covers exist (for Instagram)
  • Bio text is approved and documented for easy reference

2.9 Existing Website (If Any)

What to Look For
  • Overall visual consistency with other brand materials
  • Correct logo usage
  • Colour consistency (are the brand colours implemented correctly?)
  • Typography: are the brand fonts being used? Are they loading correctly?
  • Imagery quality and style consistency
  • Tone of voice in copy
  • Mobile responsiveness
  • Page load speed
  • SSL certificate (https)
  • Favicon present and correct
  • Open Graph meta tags (what shows when the URL is shared on social media)
  • Accessibility basics (alt text, colour contrast, keyboard navigation)
Where to Find It
  • Visit the website on both desktop and mobile
  • Use browser developer tools to inspect CSS
  • Use Google PageSpeed Insights to check performance
  • Use the WAVE accessibility tool to check accessibility
  • Share the URL on a social media platform (or use a social media debugger) to preview how it looks when shared
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Website visually matches the brand identity
  • Correct fonts are loading and rendering properly
  • Colours match the defined palette exactly
  • Imagery is high quality and follows the brand style
  • Copy is on-brand in tone
  • Technical basics are solid (fast, secure, responsive, accessible)
Needs Work
  • Website uses different colours or fonts than other materials
  • Logo is a blurry JPEG
  • Stock photos feel random and unconnected
  • No favicon, no Open Graph image
  • Slow load times, not mobile-friendly
  • No SSL certificate
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Brand colours are implemented as CSS custom properties
  • Brand fonts are loading via a proper web font strategy (Google Fonts, self-hosted WOFF2)
  • Logo is SVG (or high-resolution PNG with transparent background)
  • Favicon is present in multiple sizes (16x16, 32x32, 180x180 for Apple touch icon)
  • Open Graph meta tags are set with branded image, title, and description
  • Images are optimised and served in modern formats (WebP with fallback)
  • Site scores 80+ on Google PageSpeed Insights (mobile)
  • WCAG AA accessibility standards are met for colour contrast

2.10 Marketing Materials

What to Look For
  • Brochures, flyers, leaflets
  • Advertisements (print, digital, billboards)
  • Email marketing templates
  • Packaging (if applicable)
  • Trade show or exhibition materials
  • Promotional merchandise
  • Video content (intros, outros, lower thirds, thumbnail style)
Where to Find It
  • Ask the client for samples of everything they have produced
  • Check their email marketing platform (Mailchimp, etc.) for template history
  • Search for the brand on YouTube or social media for video content
  • Visit their physical location(s) to see printed materials
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • All materials share the same visual DNA: colours, fonts, image style, layout approach
  • Templates exist for recurring material types
  • Materials are professionally designed and produced
  • Digital materials link back to the website or landing pages effectively
Needs Work
  • Every piece looks like it was designed by a different person
  • No templates; everything is created from scratch each time
  • Low production quality
  • Outdated information, old logos, discontinued services still mentioned
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • Email marketing templates are branded and responsive
  • Digital ad templates exist in standard sizes (300x250, 728x90, 1080x1080, 1200x628, 1080x1920)
  • Video templates include branded intro/outro sequences and lower-third graphics
  • All digital materials link to correct, working URLs

2.11 Signage and Environmental Branding

What to Look For
  • Exterior signage (building signs, shop fronts)
  • Interior signage (wayfinding, wall graphics, reception area)
  • Vehicle livery
  • Uniforms or workwear
  • Packaging and labels
  • Exhibition stands or pop-up banners
Where to Find It
  • Visit the client's physical location(s) and photograph everything
  • Ask for photos if you cannot visit in person
  • Check Google Maps Street View for exterior signage
  • Look at the client's social media for photos from events
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Environmental branding matches digital and print materials
  • Signage is clear, well-maintained, and uses the correct logo/colours
  • A sense of visual continuity from the website to the front door
Needs Work
  • Signage uses an old logo or wrong colours
  • No consistency between physical and digital presence
  • Signs are faded, damaged, or poorly produced
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • High-resolution vector files exist for all signage artwork
  • Physical branding photographs are available for use on the website and social media
  • Environmental branding is consistent with the digital colour palette and typography

2.12 Internal Communications

What to Look For
  • Internal email signatures: are they branded and consistent?
  • Internal documents: proposals, reports, memos
  • Presentation templates used for internal meetings
  • Intranet or internal tools: are they branded?
  • Recruitment materials: job postings, onboarding documents
Where to Find It
  • Ask the client to share samples of internal documents
  • Look at job postings on their website or job boards
  • Ask to see a recent presentation deck
  • Ask to see a recent email signature
What "Good" Looks Like vs "Needs Work"
Good
  • Consistent email signatures across the organisation
  • Document and presentation templates that follow the brand
  • Internal communications reinforce the brand culture
Needs Work
  • Every employee has a different email signature style
  • Internal documents use default Word or PowerPoint templates
  • Job postings do not reflect the brand voice or visual identity
Digital Readiness Criteria
  • HTML email signature template exists with correct logo, colours, and layout
  • Google Docs / Microsoft Word templates exist with brand styling
  • Google Slides / PowerPoint templates exist with brand styling
  • All internal templates are stored in a shared location everyone can access
Practical Note

You will almost never encounter a client where every item on this checklist is in good shape. That is the entire point. The audit reveals gaps, and those gaps become your scope of work. The more gaps you find, the more valuable you are to the client. Never apologise for finding problems; that is your job.

Section 03

The Brand Identity Creation Process (Step by Step)

This is the full process for creating a brand identity from scratch, or for doing a complete rebrand. It works for any client, from a solo entrepreneur to a multi-division conglomerate. The scale changes; the steps do not.

Step A: Discovery and Research

What This Step Involves
Duration: 3 to 5 days Deliverable: Discovery Report

Discovery is where you learn everything you can about the client's business, their market, their audience, and their competition. This is the most important step. If you skip it or rush it, every decision you make afterwards will be guesswork.

A1. Client Interview

Schedule a 60 to 90 minute conversation. This can be in person, on a video call, or in a focused messaging session. Use these questions as your guide:

About the Business
  • What does your business do? (Get them to explain it in their own words, not from a brochure.)
  • Why did you start this business? What problem were you solving?
  • What is your business model? How do you make money?
  • What products or services generate the most revenue?
  • Where do you operate? Local, national, international?
  • How many employees do you have?
  • What are your short-term goals (next 12 months)?
  • What are your long-term goals (3 to 5 years)?
  • If your business disappeared tomorrow, what would your customers miss most?
About the Audience
  • Who is your ideal customer? Describe them as if they were a real person.
  • What is their age range, location, income level?
  • What problem do they have that you solve?
  • How do they find you? (Referrals, search, social media, advertising?)
  • What do they value most when choosing a business like yours?
  • What objections do they have before buying from you?
  • What feedback have you received from your best customers?
About the Brand
  • If your brand were a person, what three words would describe their personality?
  • How would you describe the feeling you want people to have when they interact with your business?
  • Are there any brands (in any industry) whose look and feel you admire? What do you like about them?
  • Are there any brands in your industry whose style you specifically want to avoid looking like?
  • Do you have existing brand materials? (Logo, colours, fonts, guidelines)
  • What works about your current branding? What does not?
  • Are there any colours, symbols, or visual themes that are meaningful to your business or industry?

A2. Competitor Analysis

Identify the client's top 5 to 8 direct competitors and 3 to 5 aspirational competitors (businesses they admire or want to be compared to). For each competitor, document:

  • Visual identity: Logo, colours, typography, overall style
  • Website: Quality, user experience, content approach
  • Social media: Platforms used, content style, engagement level
  • Positioning: What they claim to be best at, who they target
  • Strengths: What they do well that the client can learn from
  • Gaps: What they do poorly that the client can capitalise on

Arrange your findings in a competitor landscape map. Plot competitors on a simple 2x2 grid using two dimensions relevant to the industry (for example, "traditional vs modern" and "premium vs affordable"). This helps you visually identify white space where the client can position themselves distinctively.

A3. Market and Industry Research

  • Look at industry trends: What visual and verbal trends are common? What is overdone?
  • Check cultural considerations: Are there colours, symbols, or phrases that have specific meaning in the client's market?
  • Understand the client's industry lifecycle: Is it mature and established, or emerging and evolving?
  • Review the client's customer reviews and testimonials for language patterns

Deliverable: Discovery Report

Compile everything into a written document. It does not need to be fancy. A clean Google Doc or Notion page is fine. Include:

  • Summary of client interview insights
  • Audience profiles (1 to 3 key audience segments)
  • Competitor analysis with screenshots and positioning map
  • Key observations and opportunities
  • Initial direction recommendations (2 to 3 possible positioning angles)

Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Skipping the interview and going straight to design. You will waste time creating work the client does not connect with.
  • Only interviewing the owner. If possible, also talk to employees who interact with customers and to actual customers.
  • Only looking at direct competitors. Some of the best inspiration comes from businesses in completely different industries that share a similar audience or positioning.
  • Not documenting your findings. If it is not written down, it is not usable. Write everything up even if the client does not ask for a formal report.

Step B: Brand Strategy

What This Step Involves
Duration: 2 to 4 days Deliverable: Brand Strategy Document

Brand strategy translates your research into decisions. This is where you define the brand's fundamental direction before touching any visual design. Think of it as writing the creative brief for yourself.

B1. Mission Statement

The mission answers: Why does this business exist, and who does it serve?

Keep it to one or two sentences. It should be specific enough to guide decisions but broad enough to allow growth.

Good Example

"We provide Nigerian farmers with reliable agricultural inputs and market access, helping them increase yields and income through every season."

Too Vague

"We are committed to excellence in agriculture and related services across Nigeria and beyond."

B2. Vision Statement

The vision answers: What does success look like in 5 to 10 years?

This is aspirational. It describes a future state, not the current one.

Good Example

"To be West Africa's most trusted integrated agricultural and commodity business, known for quality, reliability, and impact in every community we serve."

Too Generic

"To be the leading company in our industry."

B3. Brand Values

Values answer: What principles guide our behaviour and decisions?

Choose 3 to 5 values. For each value, write a short explanation of what it means in practice, not just the word itself.

Example: Values with Definitions

Reliability — We deliver what we promise, when we promise it. Our word is our bond in every transaction, from a bag of fertiliser to a real estate contract.

Impact — We measure success not just in profit but in the tangible difference we make in farmers' yields, communities' livelihoods, and Nigeria's economy.

Integrity — We conduct business transparently and fairly. We would rather lose a deal than lose trust.

Growth — We invest in continuous improvement, in our people, our processes, and our partners.

B4. Brand Positioning Statement

A positioning statement defines where the brand sits in the market relative to competitors. Use this template:

Positioning Statement Template

For [target audience], [Brand Name] is the [category] that [key benefit/differentiator] because [reason to believe].


Example: For Nigerian agricultural businesses and commodity buyers, [Conglomerate Name] is the integrated agricultural enterprise that provides end-to-end solutions from farm to market, because we operate across the entire value chain with owned infrastructure in crop farming, processing, marketing, and export.

B5. Target Audience Definition

Create 1 to 3 audience profiles. For each profile, document:

  • Demographics: Age, gender, location, income, education, occupation
  • Psychographics: Values, interests, lifestyle, attitudes
  • Pain points: What problems or frustrations do they face?
  • Goals: What are they trying to achieve?
  • Decision factors: What matters most when they choose a provider?
  • Information sources: Where do they look for information? Who do they trust?
  • Digital behaviour: Which platforms do they use? How tech-savvy are they?

Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Writing a mission statement that could belong to any company. If you can swap in a competitor's name and it still works, it is too generic.
  • Choosing values that are just table-stakes expectations. "Quality" and "customer service" are not differentiators. Every business claims those.
  • Skipping brand personality. Without a defined personality, visual and verbal decisions become arbitrary. "Should the photo be warm or cool-toned?" is impossible to answer without knowing the brand personality.
  • Creating strategy in isolation. Get the client's sign-off on the strategy document before moving to visual design. If the strategy is wrong, the design will be wrong.

Step C: Visual Identity Development

What This Step Involves
Duration: 5 to 10 days Tools: Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Canva Pro Deliverables: Logo suite, colour palette, type system, visual language

Now you design. But because you have done the research and strategy, you are not guessing. Every visual decision connects back to the brand personality, the target audience, and the competitive positioning.

C1. Mood Board / Visual Direction

Before designing the logo, create 2 to 3 mood boards showing different visual directions the brand could go. Each mood board should include:

  • Colour inspiration (5 to 8 colour swatches)
  • Typography samples (2 to 3 font pairings)
  • Photography references (5 to 8 images showing the mood and style)
  • Texture and pattern references
  • Existing brand examples that share a similar feel
  • A brief written description of each direction and how it connects to the brand strategy

Present these to the client and get them to choose a direction (or a combination) before you start logo design. This prevents you from going down a path they do not connect with.

C2. Logo Design Process

  1. Sketching (Day 1 to 2): Start with pencil and paper. Generate at least 30 to 50 rough concepts. Do not judge them yet. The goal is volume. Explore different approaches: wordmarks, lettermarks, abstract symbols, pictorial marks, combinations.
  2. Shortlisting (Day 2): Review your sketches and select the 8 to 12 strongest concepts. Consider: Does it connect to the brand strategy? Is it distinctive? Will it work at small sizes? Is it timeless rather than trendy?
  3. Digital refinement (Day 3 to 5): Digitise your shortlisted concepts in a vector tool (Illustrator or Figma). Refine the geometry, spacing, and proportions. Create clean, precise versions of each concept.
  4. Testing (Day 5 to 6): Test each concept at different sizes (favicon, social media avatar, business card, billboard). Test on light and dark backgrounds. Test in single colour. Test in greyscale.
  5. Presentation (Day 6 to 7): Present 2 to 3 final concepts to the client. Show each concept in realistic contexts (on a business card, on a website header, on a social media post). Never present a logo floating on a blank white page.
  6. Refinement (Day 7 to 10): Based on client feedback, refine the chosen concept. Adjust proportions, colours, spacing. Create all required variations.
Logo Variations You Need to Create
  • Primary logo: The main version, used most often
  • Secondary logo: Alternative layout (if the primary is horizontal, the secondary might be stacked)
  • Logomark only: Just the icon/symbol without text
  • Logotype only: Just the wordmark without the icon
  • Single-colour versions: All black, all white
  • Reverse version: For use on dark backgrounds
  • Favicon version: Simplified for 16x16 to 32x32 pixel display

Building Your Palette
  1. Start with 1 to 2 primary colours. These are the colours people will associate with the brand. They should reflect the brand personality and be distinctive from major competitors.
  2. Add 1 to 2 secondary colours. These support the primary colours and add variety without competing for attention.
  3. Choose 1 accent colour. This is a standout colour used sparingly for calls to action, highlights, and interactive elements. It should have strong contrast with the primary colours.
  4. Define neutrals. You need at least: a near-black for text, a dark grey for secondary text, a medium grey for borders/dividers, a light grey for backgrounds, and white.
  5. Create tints and shades. For each primary, secondary, and accent colour, create a scale from light (10%) to dark (90%) in 10% increments. This gives you a rich palette for backgrounds, hover states, borders, and subtle variations.

C4. Typography Selection

Choose 2 to 3 typefaces (rarely more). Here is how to approach it:

Font Roles
  • Display/heading font: Creates personality and visual impact. Can be more distinctive. Used for headings, titles, hero text.
  • Body font: Prioritises readability above all else. Used for paragraphs, lists, general content. Must be legible at 16px on screen.
  • UI/accent font (optional): For buttons, labels, navigation, captions. Often the body font in a different weight, or a clean sans-serif if the body font is a serif.
Font Pairing Principles
  • Contrast, not conflict. A serif heading with a sans-serif body creates pleasant contrast. Two very different sans-serifs can clash.
  • Shared qualities. Look for fonts that share x-height proportions, letter width, or design era, even if they are different classifications.
  • Test with real content. Do not judge a font pairing using "Lorem ipsum." Use real text from the client's industry.
  • Limit weights. Each font weight is an additional file to load. Choose 2 to 3 weights per typeface (regular, medium or semibold, bold). Avoid loading every weight from 100 to 900.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Designing a logo that only works in one format. If your logo needs colour to function, it will fail in fax, photocopy, and single-colour embroidery contexts.
  • Choosing a trendy colour palette. Trends fade. A brand identity needs to last 5 to 10 years minimum. Choose colours that feel current but not fadish.
  • Using too many typefaces. Two is almost always enough. Three is the maximum. More than that creates visual chaos.
  • Presenting too many options. Three logo concepts is ideal. More than that overwhelms the client and leads to "franken-logos" where they want to combine elements from different concepts.
  • Not testing at small sizes. A logo that looks great on a poster but becomes an unreadable blob at 32px is not a good logo.

Step D: Tone of Voice Development

What This Step Involves
Duration: 1 to 2 days Deliverable: Tone of Voice Guide

Tone of voice is the verbal equivalent of visual identity. It is how the brand sounds in every piece of written communication. Many designers skip this. Do not be one of them. A beautifully designed brand that sounds generic in its copy is only half-finished.

D1. Define Voice Traits

Choose 3 to 4 traits that describe how the brand communicates. For each trait, use a "this, not that" format to make it practical:

Example: Voice Traits for an Agricultural Conglomerate

Confident, not arrogant. We speak with authority because we know our industry. But we never talk down to people or dismiss smaller players. We lead by example, not by lecturing.

Warm, not casual. We are approachable and human. We use friendly language. But we maintain professionalism. We are not your mate at the pub; we are your trusted business partner who happens to be easy to talk to.

Clear, not oversimplified. We explain complex topics in straightforward language. But we do not "dumb things down." We respect our audience's intelligence while making information accessible.

Grounded, not old-fashioned. We value tradition, experience, and proven methods. But we embrace new technology and modern approaches. We are rooted in the land but reaching for innovation.

D2. Writing Guidelines

  • Sentence length: Prefer short to medium sentences (15 to 25 words). Mix lengths for rhythm.
  • Paragraph length: Keep paragraphs to 3 to 4 sentences for digital content.
  • Vocabulary: Use industry terms where the audience expects them, but always explain jargon when writing for a general audience.
  • Person: Do we say "we" (first person plural), "the company" (third person), or address the reader as "you" (second person)?
  • Contractions: Are contractions OK (we're, it's, you'll) or does the brand prefer full forms (we are, it is, you will)?
  • Humour: How much, if any? What type?

D3. Word Lists

Words We Use
  • Partner (not vendor)
  • Community (not market)
  • Invest (not spend)
  • Build (not develop)
  • Opportunity (not problem)
  • Reliable (not cheap)
  • Proven (not innovative, unless it truly is)
Words We Avoid
  • Synergy, leverage, paradigm (corporate jargon)
  • Cheap, discount, budget (undermines value)
  • Best, number one, leading (unless provable)
  • Disruption (overused)
  • Solutions (vague; say what the solution actually is)
  • Utilize (just say "use")

D4. Tone Variations by Context

The voice stays the same, but the tone shifts by context:

ContextTone AdjustmentExample
Website homepage Confident, inspiring, concise "From seed to shelf, we grow Nigeria's future."
Social media Warmer, conversational, engaging "This morning at the Kano processing facility. 200 tonnes of grain, sorted and ready for export. This is what reliable looks like."
Formal proposal Authoritative, detailed, precise "Our integrated approach encompasses seven verticals, ensuring quality control from primary production through to international export."
Error message Honest, helpful, not blaming "Something went wrong on our end. Please try again, or contact us at support@example.com and we will sort it out."
Email newsletter Informative, friendly, value-first "Three things you should know about the groundnut market this month."
Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Writing tone guidelines that are too abstract. "We are professional" tells the writer nothing. Use "this, not that" examples so people can actually apply the guidelines.
  • Ignoring digital-specific contexts. How should the brand sound in a chatbot response? In a push notification? In an SMS? These micro-copy moments matter.
  • Not providing enough examples. For each guideline, include at least one "before and after" showing how generic copy becomes on-brand copy.

Section 04

Brand Architecture for Conglomerates

Brand architecture is how you organise multiple brands, divisions, or product lines within a single business. For a conglomerate with 7 verticals (Crop Farming, Animal Husbandry, Agro-Processing, Commodity Marketing, Real Estate, Import & Export, Oil & Gas), this is one of the most important strategic decisions you will make.

The Four Architecture Models

1. Branded House (Monolithic)

Structure: One master brand. All divisions use the same name, same logo, same visual identity. The divisions may have descriptive names but not independent identities.

How it looks:
[Company] Crop Farming
[Company] Animal Husbandry
[Company] Real Estate
[Company] Oil & Gas

Real-world examples:

  • Virgin: Virgin Atlantic, Virgin Media, Virgin Money, Virgin Active. All share the Virgin name, logo, and red colour. The brand promise of "disrupting the industry with style" carries across every division.
  • Google: Google Search, Google Maps, Google Drive, Google Cloud. One brand, many products. All benefit from the trust and recognition of the Google name.
  • FedEx: FedEx Express, FedEx Ground, FedEx Freight. The master brand is consistent; colour coding differentiates the services.

Advantages:

  • Maximum brand equity. Every division benefits from the reputation of the master brand.
  • Efficient. One brand to build, one identity to manage, one set of guidelines.
  • Clear market message: this is one powerful, integrated company.
  • Lower marketing costs because you are only promoting one brand.

Disadvantages:

  • Risk concentration. If one division has a scandal, the entire brand is affected.
  • Can feel generic if the divisions serve very different audiences.
  • Difficult if divisions need distinctly different brand personalities.

2. House of Brands (Pluralistic)

Structure: Each division or product has its own independent brand. The parent company may be invisible to consumers.

Real-world examples:

  • Procter & Gamble: Tide, Gillette, Pampers, Oral-B. Most consumers do not know or care that P&G owns these brands. Each brand has its own identity, personality, and target audience.
  • Unilever: Dove, Lipton, Ben & Jerry's, Hellmann's. Completely independent brand identities.
  • LVMH: Louis Vuitton, Dior, Fendi, Bulgari. Each luxury brand maintains its own heritage and identity.

Advantages:

  • Each brand can target a completely different audience with an authentic voice.
  • Risk is isolated. A problem with one brand does not contaminate the others.
  • Allows for acquisitions without forcing a rebrand.

Disadvantages:

  • Expensive. Each brand needs its own identity, guidelines, marketing budget.
  • No shared brand equity. Each brand starts from zero in terms of recognition.
  • Complex to manage, especially for smaller organisations.

3. Endorsed Brands

Structure: Each division has its own brand identity, but it is visibly endorsed by the parent company. The endorsement gives each sub-brand credibility while allowing individual personality.

How it looks:
[Division Name] — a [Company] company
or
[Division Name] by [Company]
or
[Division Name] with a small [Company] logo

Real-world examples:

  • Marriott International: Courtyard by Marriott, Residence Inn by Marriott, The Ritz-Carlton (a Marriott International hotel). Each hotel brand has its own personality, but the Marriott endorsement signals quality standards.
  • Nestlé: KitKat has its own identity but often shows the Nestlé seal. Nescafé is endorsed by the Nestlé name.
  • Polo Ralph Lauren: Ralph Lauren, Polo, Lauren, RLX. Different lines endorsed by the parent brand.

Advantages:

  • Sub-brands benefit from parent credibility while maintaining individuality.
  • Balanced risk. A problem with one sub-brand is somewhat contained.
  • Allows differentiation for different audiences and markets.

Disadvantages:

  • More complex than a branded house. You need to design the relationship between the parent identity and each sub-brand.
  • The endorsement must be consistent, or it looks arbitrary.

4. Hybrid

Structure: A combination of the above approaches. Some divisions use the master brand, some have endorsed brands, some are independent. This is common with large conglomerates that have grown through acquisition.

Real-world examples:

  • Alphabet/Google: Google products use the Google brand (branded house), while other Alphabet companies (Waymo, Verily, Calico) are independent brands (house of brands).
  • Amazon: Amazon Retail, Amazon Web Services, and Amazon Prime are branded house. But Ring, Whole Foods, and Twitch are endorsed or independent brands.
  • Dangote Group: Dangote Cement, Dangote Sugar, Dangote Salt use the master brand (branded house), but some investments operate independently.

Decision Framework: Which Architecture Is Right?

Use this framework to guide the decision. Score each criterion for the client's situation:

CriterionPoints to Branded HousePoints to House of Brands

Quality consistency All divisions deliver similar quality Quality varies significantly across divisions
Brand personality All divisions share the same values and personality Divisions need fundamentally different personalities
Budget Limited marketing budget Large marketing budget for each brand
Team size Small team managing all communications Each division has its own marketing team
Risk tolerance Low-risk industries; reputation damage is unlikely Some divisions are in high-risk industries (Oil & Gas, for example)

1. Define the Visual Relationship

  • Flexible elements: What changes per sub-brand? (Typically: a secondary colour unique to each division, division-specific photography style, potentially a division icon or symbol)

  • Division name in the brand typography with the division colour, master logo as a small endorsement mark in the corner

4. Define the Rules

  • When does a division use the master brand lock-up vs. just the master brand logo?
  • How much creative freedom does each division have within the system?
  • Which elements are non-negotiable (these go in the "sacred" section of the guidelines)?
  • Which elements can be adapted (these go in the "flexible" section)?

Maintaining Coherence Across Divisions

The biggest challenge with multi-division brands is preventing each division from slowly drifting into its own visual world. Here is how to prevent that:

  • Shared typography: All divisions use the same heading and body fonts. No exceptions.
  • Shared grid and layout system: A consistent approach to spacing, margins, and content structure.
  • Master colour always present: The primary brand colour should appear somewhere in every division's materials, even if just in the header or footer.
  • Centralised approval: Designate one person or team who reviews all brand materials before publication.
  • Shared asset library: All divisions access templates, logos, and files from the same central location.
  • Regular brand audits: Every quarter, review each division's recent materials for consistency.
Recommendation for the Current Client

For an agricultural conglomerate with 7 verticals, I recommend the Endorsed Brand model. The parent company brand gives credibility and signals the breadth of the operation, while each division gets a unique colour and minimal visual distinction to help audiences identify the right service. The divisions are related enough in audience and values that a completely independent "house of brands" would be wasteful, but different enough that a pure "branded house" would feel forced, especially when Oil & Gas sits alongside Crop Farming. An endorsed model with colour coding gives you the best of both worlds.

Pricing Brand Identity Work

Pricing branding work can feel uncertain when you are new to it. The key is to understand the value you are delivering and to package your services clearly so clients know exactly what they are getting. These guidelines are for the Nigerian market (and similar markets) but can be adapted by adjusting the multipliers for your target market.

8.1 Brand Audit

What Is Included

  • Review of all existing brand assets (the full checklist from Section 2)
  • Competitor landscape analysis (5 to 8 competitors)
  • Scoring and assessment of each brand area
  • Written report with findings, gap analysis, and prioritised recommendations
  • Presentation of findings to the client (30 to 60 minutes)

Effort

Typically 2 to 4 days of focused work (research, documentation, writing).

Pricing Approach

A brand audit is a standalone service that demonstrates your expertise and naturally leads to more work. Price it at a level that is accessible enough to get your foot in the door, but high enough to be taken seriously.

8.4 Bundling with Web Design

Bundling brand identity work with a website project is the most natural approach for web designers, and it often makes the most sense for the client too.

Package Structures

Option A: Brand Identity + Website (Integrated Package)

When to use: The client needs both a new brand identity and a new website. The most common scenario for new businesses or complete rebrands.

How to price: Add the brand identity cost to your website cost. Offer a 10% to 15% discount on the combined price as an incentive for bundling. The discount is justified because you gain efficiency from doing both, and you guarantee yourself the web project.

Example: If your website price is X and brand identity is 0.75X, the bundle would be 1.75X minus 10% = 1.575X. The client saves money, you earn more than a website-only project, and you deliver a more cohesive result.

Option B: Brand Foundation + Website (Essentials Package)

When to use: The client's budget is limited but they still need branding. You include the essential brand work (logo, colours, typography, basic guidelines) as part of the website project, because you need these to design a good website anyway.

How to price: Add 25% to 35% to your standard website price. Frame it as "the website project includes brand foundation work" rather than itemising it separately.

Example: A standard website at X becomes 1.3X with brand foundation included. The client gets a website that is backed by a thought-through identity system, and you get paid for the branding work you would have done anyway.

Option C: Phased Approach

When to use: Large projects, enterprise clients, or situations where the brand needs to be approved before website work begins.

How to price: Phase 1 (Brand Identity) and Phase 2 (Website) are separately scoped and priced. Each phase has its own contract and deliverables. Phase 2 is contingent on Phase 1 completion.

Advantage: Reduces risk for both sides. The client can evaluate your branding work before committing to the website. You get paid for Phase 1 regardless of whether Phase 2 happens.

8.5 When to Charge Separately vs Include

ScenarioRecommendation
Client has no brand identity at all Charge separately. This is significant work that should be visible and valued as its own deliverable.
Client has a logo but nothing else Bundle with website. Include colour documentation, typography setup, and basic guidelines as part of the web design process. Add 20% to 30% to the project price.
Client has a comprehensive brand identity Include a light audit in the web project. Review existing brand assets as part of your normal design process. No additional charge unless significant issues are found.
Client wants a brand audit but is not ready for a website Charge separately. The audit is a standalone service. Use it as a foot-in-the-door for future web design work.
Multi-division client (like the current project) Always charge separately. Brand architecture for multiple divisions is complex, valuable work. It must be its own line item with its own scope.
Important

Never do significant brand identity work for free and bury it inside a web design project. If you are making strategic decisions about brand positioning, designing a logo, or creating guidelines, that work has value and should be visible in the proposal. Clients need to understand what they are paying for and what they are getting.

8.6 Communicating Value to Clients

When presenting branding costs to a client, focus on outcomes rather than activities:

  • Instead of: "Logo design includes 3 concepts with 2 rounds of revisions"
  • Try: "You will have a professional logo system that works across your website, social media, business cards, signage, and any future marketing. It includes all the file formats you will ever need, so you will never have to pay to convert or recreate your logo."
  • Instead of: "Brand guidelines document, approximately 30 pages"
  • Try: "You will have a brand instruction manual that ensures your business looks consistent and professional no matter who creates materials for you, whether that is a social media manager, a printer, or a future web developer."
  • Instead of: "Colour palette with hex codes and RGB values"
  • Try: "Your colours will be documented so precisely that any designer, developer, or printer can reproduce them exactly. No more guessing, no more slightly-different-blues across your materials."

10.3 How to Handle 7 Divisions Within One Brand System

Seven divisions is a lot. Here is how to keep it manageable:

Prioritise by Business Importance

Not all 7 divisions are equal. Some are likely the primary revenue drivers; others are smaller or newer. Ask the client to rank the divisions by:

  1. Current revenue contribution
  2. Strategic importance (future growth plans)
  3. Public-facing visibility (how much does this division interact with customers directly?)

Design the top 3 to 4 divisions in full detail. Create the remaining divisions using the same system but with less custom attention. You can always expand them later.

Create a Modular System, Not 7 Custom Brands

The endorsed brand model means you are creating one system with 7 colour-coded variants, not 7 separate brands. In practice, this means:

  • One logo structure with a variable division name and colour
  • One set of typography rules used everywhere
  • One layout and grid system applied to all divisions
  • One tone of voice with minor adjustments per division
  • Division-specific elements limited to: colour accent, photography style, and (maybe) a division icon

10.4 Recommended Brand Architecture for This Client

Based on the profile of this client (a Nigerian conglomerate with 7 related but distinct verticals), here is the recommended approach:

Recommended: Endorsed Brand Architecture

Master brand: [Conglomerate Name] with its own logo, primary colours (navy and gold), and full visual identity.

Division brands: Each division carries the master brand endorsement through a consistent lockup. The division name appears alongside the master logo, and the division's unique colour is used as an accent throughout that division's materials.

Rationale:

  • The divisions share a common owner and, in most cases, serve overlapping customer segments (agricultural businesses, commodity buyers, Nigerian business professionals).
  • The master brand's credibility in agriculture (crop farming, animal husbandry, processing) can boost trust in the newer or less-established divisions (real estate, import/export).
  • The client likely has one marketing team (or a very small one), which makes managing 7 independent brands unrealistic.
  • The budget is better spent building one strong brand than spreading across 7 weak ones.
  • Oil & Gas is the division most different from the others. If the client wants to distance this division from the agricultural core, it can be the one exception: a more independent brand with a subtle endorsement. But start with the endorsed model for all and evaluate later.

How it works in practice:

  1. The website has one domain with a unified navigation. Each division is a section of the site, not a separate site.
  2. When a division creates a social media post, it uses the master brand templates with the division colour as the accent.
  3. Business cards carry the master brand logo and the division name, in the division colour.
  4. Proposals and formal documents use the master brand letterhead. The division name appears on the first page.
  5. Marketing materials can be adapted with the division colour to signal which part of the business is being promoted.

10.6 Final Advice for This Project

A few parting thoughts specific to this client:

  • Start with the master brand. Get the parent company identity locked in before you touch the divisions. The divisions are built on top of the master brand, so it must be solid.
  • Do not over-design the divisions. At this stage, each division needs a colour, a name in the lockup, and a photography direction. That is enough. You can expand each division's identity later as the business grows.
  • Document everything as you go. Do not save the guidelines document for the end. Start it on Day 1 and add to it every day. By the end, you will have a comprehensive reference rather than a rushed afterthought.
  • The website design phase will be easier because of this work. Every colour, font, button style, and content tone will already be decided. You will be assembling pages from a design system rather than making decisions on the fly. That is the payoff.
  • This is a portfolio-defining project. A 7-division conglomerate brand identity is impressive work. Document your process, screenshot your deliverables, and write a case study when it is done. This one project can attract similar clients for years.
