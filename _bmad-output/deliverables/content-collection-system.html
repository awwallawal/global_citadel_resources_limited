1 Content Collection Overview

Why This System Exists

Your website is being built around your content, not the other way around. Every design decision, every layout choice, and every interactive feature depends on having the right words, images, and information in place at the right time. This document is a structured system for collecting that content from you efficiently, completely, and on schedule.

Building a website for a conglomerate with seven distinct divisions is a significant undertaking. The sheer volume of content required is substantial: company-wide information, seven unique division profiles, team bios, case studies, photography, legal pages, and more. Without a clear system, content collection becomes the single biggest risk to the project timeline.

What Happens When Content Is Late or Disorganised

Real consequences of content delays

  • Design delays: Page layouts cannot be finalised with placeholder text. "Lorem ipsum" designs hide problems that only appear when real content is inserted. Paragraphs that are too long break layouts. Headings that are too short look weak. Real content must drive design.
  • Compounding schedule slippage: Every week of content delay pushes development back by at least that same week, and often more. A two-week content delay does not mean a two-week project delay. It typically means three to four weeks because other scheduled work must be re-sequenced.
  • Rush-quality content: When content arrives at the last minute, it arrives in poor shape. Descriptions are vague, bios are incomplete, photos are low resolution or taken on a phone in bad lighting. This content goes live on your website representing your business to every visitor.
  • Increased costs: The project agreement includes provisions for delays caused by late content. Extended timelines mean extended commitment from both sides, and may trigger additional charges as outlined in your service agreement.
  • Missed launch dates: The most visible consequence. Stakeholders, partners, and customers who have been told to expect the new site are left waiting.

How This System Prevents Problems

This system works by breaking the enormous task of "provide all your website content" into smaller, manageable pieces with clear descriptions of what is needed, who is responsible, and when it is due. Specifically:

  • The Master Content Inventory (Section 2) lists every single piece of content needed, so nothing is forgotten or discovered at the last minute.
  • The Image Requirements (Section 3) specify exactly what photographs are needed and how to take or provide them, eliminating back-and-forth about quality and format.
  • The Content Brief Templates (Section 4) give you ready-to-fill forms, so you never have to wonder what to write or how much to write.
  • The Shared Folder Structure (Section 5) ensures every file has a clear home, preventing the chaos of content scattered across email threads, WhatsApp messages, and random file shares.
  • The Delivery Schedule (Section 6) connects content deadlines to project milestones, so you can see exactly why each deadline matters.
  • The Quality Guidelines (Section 7) help you provide content that is web-ready, reducing the rounds of revision needed.

Your role in this process

You know your business better than anyone. No web designer can write your company history, describe your services, or capture the spirit of your operations. Your active participation in content collection is essential. This system makes that participation as straightforward as possible, but the content itself must come from you and your team.

How to Use This Document

  1. Read Section 2 to understand the full scope of content needed. Assign responsible people from your team to each category.
  2. Review Section 3 and begin planning photography immediately. Good photos take time to organise.
  3. Use the templates in Section 4 to draft content. Fill in the blanks, save as Word documents or Google Docs, and upload to the shared folder.
  4. Set up the shared folder (Section 5) early and use it consistently. Everything in one place.
  5. Follow the schedule in Section 6 closely. Treat deadlines as non-negotiable.

3 Image and Photography Requirements

Photography is one of the most impactful elements of any website. The difference between a website with authentic, high-quality photography and one relying on generic images is immediately obvious to visitors. This section provides everything you need to plan, capture, and deliver the photographs required for the project.

Technical Requirements

Minimum Specifications for All Photos

  • Resolution: Minimum 2000 pixels wide. For hero images and full-width backgrounds, 2400px or wider is preferred. Height should be proportional to the composition.
  • File format: JPG (JPEG) for all photographs. PNG for logos, graphics, or any image requiring a transparent background.
  • File size: Aim for 1-5 MB per photo. Files under 500 KB are likely too compressed and will appear blurry. Files over 10 MB are unnecessarily large.
  • Colour space: sRGB (the default for most cameras and phones).
  • Orientation: Landscape (horizontal) orientation is preferred for most website uses. Portrait (vertical) is acceptable for team headshots and specific gallery images.
  • Headshots: Minimum 800x800 pixels. Square or portrait orientation. Head and shoulders framing. Plain or neutral background. Even, flattering lighting with no harsh shadows.

No Stock Photos Policy

Stock photos are not acceptable

Every photograph on your website must be authentic, meaning it must show your actual operations, facilities, people, and products. Stock photography (generic images purchased from image libraries) will not be used on this project for the following reasons:

  • Credibility: Visitors can identify stock photos instantly. They signal that a company either does not have real operations worth photographing or does not care enough to show them. For a conglomerate with seven active divisions, this would be a missed opportunity.
  • Trust: Authentic photos build trust. When a potential partner sees your actual farm, your real team, and your genuine facilities, they know they are dealing with a real, operational business.
  • Differentiation: Your competitors may use the same stock photos. Your authentic photos are uniquely yours and cannot appear on any other website.
  • Legal risk: Stock photo licensing is complex. Authentic photos that you own outright carry no licensing risks.

If professional photography cannot be arranged, high-quality photos taken with a modern smartphone (2020 or newer) are acceptable, provided the guidelines below are followed.

Tips for Taking Good Photos Without a Professional Photographer

Lighting

Natural light is your best friend. Shoot outdoors in the early morning (7-9 AM) or late afternoon (4-6 PM) when the light is warm and soft. Avoid midday sun, which creates harsh shadows. Indoors, position subjects near large windows. Never shoot with a bright light source (window or sun) directly behind your subject.

Stability

Hold the phone or camera with both hands and brace your elbows against your body. Better yet, use a tripod or rest the camera on a stable surface. Blurry photos from camera shake are the most common problem and cannot be fixed afterwards.

Composition

Use the "rule of thirds": imagine the frame divided into a 3x3 grid and place key subjects along the grid lines or at their intersections. Most phone cameras have a grid overlay option in settings. Leave some space around your subject rather than cropping too tightly.

Clean the Scene

Before shooting, remove distracting elements: clutter, rubbish, personal items, brand logos of competitors, or anything that does not represent the company positively. Make sure uniforms are clean, safety gear is worn properly, and work areas are tidy.

Multiple Angles

Take each shot from at least three different angles and distances (wide, medium, close-up). Take more photos than you think you need. It is far easier to choose from 20 photos than to reshoot because none of the original 5 worked.

People in Photos

Include people in operational shots to add scale and human interest. People should be actively doing something (not posing awkwardly for the camera). Ensure everyone is wearing appropriate attire and safety gear for the setting. Obtain verbal or written consent from every person photographed.

Phone Camera Settings

Shoot at the highest resolution available. Do not use digital zoom (it degrades quality; move closer instead). Turn off beauty filters and HDR modes for operational shots. Clean the camera lens with a soft cloth before shooting. Use the main camera, not the selfie camera, for all non-headshot photos.

Headshot Tips

Have the subject stand facing a window or in open shade outdoors. Use a plain wall as the background (white, grey, or a neutral colour). Frame from the chest up. Have them look directly at the camera with a natural, relaxed expression. Take at least 10 shots to get one good one.

7 Content Quality Guidelines

Good website content is not about fancy writing. It is about clear, purposeful communication that helps your visitors find what they need and take action. The following guidelines will help you write content that works well on the web and reduces the number of revision rounds needed.

Write for Your Audience, Not for Yourself

Before writing any content, ask: "Who will read this, and what do they need to know?" A potential investor reads differently from a farmer looking to buy seeds. A job seeker reads differently from a bulk commodity buyer. Tailor your language, tone, and detail level to the person you most want to reach on each page.

Keep Paragraphs Short

On a screen, long paragraphs are intimidating walls of text. Most visitors scan rather than read word-by-word. Limit paragraphs to 3-4 sentences maximum. Break complex ideas into multiple short paragraphs. Use bullet points for lists of items, features, or benefits. White space is your friend.

Lead With the Most Important Information

Put the conclusion first, then the supporting details. Most visitors will not read past the first sentence of a section. If your key message is buried in the third paragraph, most people will never see it. Open every section with the single most important thing the reader should know.

Use Specific Numbers, Not Vague Claims

Vague claims like "we serve many farmers" or "we have extensive experience" say nothing and convince no one. Specific numbers are compelling and credible. Write "Serving 500+ farmers across 12 states" instead. Write "15 years of continuous operation" instead of "many years of experience." If you have the numbers, use them.

Avoid Jargon Unless Your Audience Expects It

Industry terminology can make you sound knowledgeable to insiders, but it confuses everyone else. Use plain language for general pages (Homepage, About, Contact). Use technical terms only on division-specific pages where the audience is likely to be industry professionals. When in doubt, choose the simpler word.

Every Page Needs a Clear Purpose and Call to Action

Before writing content for any page, answer two questions: "What is the purpose of this page?" and "What do I want the visitor to do after reading it?" The purpose shapes what you write. The call to action tells the visitor what to do next: contact you, request a quote, explore a division, apply for a job, or learn more.

Write in Active Voice

Active voice is more direct and engaging. Write "Our team cultivates 5,000 hectares of farmland" instead of "5,000 hectares of farmland are cultivated by our team." Active voice tells the reader who is doing what, making the content stronger and easier to understand.

Proofread Everything

Spelling errors, grammatical mistakes, and inconsistencies undermine credibility. Before submitting any content, read it aloud to catch awkward phrasing. Have a second person review it. Check that company names, job titles, and statistics are consistent across all documents. If in doubt, provide raw information and Awwal can help refine the writing.

8 Chasing Content (Internal Guide)

INTERNAL USE ONLY - FOR LAWAL AWWAL - NOT FOR CLIENT DISTRIBUTION

Content chasing is an inevitable part of freelance web design. This section provides a systematic approach to following up on late content, protecting yourself contractually, and maintaining the client relationship throughout the process.

Follow-Up Timeline

Timing Action Tone Method
3 days before deadline First reminder Friendly, helpful Email + WhatsApp message
On deadline day Second reminder Professional, firmer Email (creates paper trail)
3 days after deadline Escalation notice Formal, references contract Email only (documented)
7 days after deadline Impact assessment Formal, outlines consequences Email with revised timeline
14 days after deadline Project pause consideration Firm but professional Formal email, reference pause clause

As a reminder, you can upload everything to the shared folder here: [SHARED FOLDER LINK]

When to Invoke the Project Pause Clause

The pause clause is a last resort. Before invoking it, ensure all of the following are true:

  1. You have a documented trail: All reminder emails have been sent on schedule and you have records (sent emails, read receipts where possible).
  2. The delay is significant: At least 14 calendar days past the deadline, and the delay is blocking meaningful progress on the project.
  3. You have offered alternatives: You have offered calls, simplified requirements, extended mini-deadlines, and the client has not responded or has not followed through.
  4. Your contract supports it: The service agreement contains a clause that permits pausing work due to client delays. If it does not, you cannot invoke this. Instead, document the delays and negotiate.
  5. You are prepared for the consequence: A pause may damage the client relationship. Weigh this against the cost of indefinite waiting. If the client is a major account or a referral source, consider whether a more flexible approach (extra reminders, in-person visit, offering to write content from interview notes) would be more strategic.

How to Document Delivery Status

Maintain a private tracking spreadsheet (not shared with the client) that records the following for every content item:

Column What to Record
Content Item The specific item name, matching the Master Content Inventory in Section 2.
Division / Category Which division or page this content belongs to.
Original Due Date The date from the agreed schedule.
Reminder 1 Sent Date you sent the first reminder (3 days before deadline). Leave blank if not yet applicable.
Reminder 2 Sent Date you sent the second reminder (on deadline day).
Escalation Sent Date you sent the escalation email (3 days after deadline). Leave blank if content arrived on time.
Date Received The actual date the content was uploaded to the shared folder or otherwise delivered.
Days Late Number of days between the due date and the received date. 0 if on time. Negative if early.
Quality Status "Usable as-is", "Needs minor edits", "Needs major revision", or "Unusable - must redo". Note what the issues are.
Notes Any relevant context: who provided it, verbal commitments made, reasons given for delay, or quality issues found.

Update this spreadsheet at least twice per week during active content collection phases. This record serves three purposes:

  1. Self-protection: If a dispute arises about whether content was delivered or when, you have a complete record.
  2. Project management: You can see at a glance which items are blocking progress and prioritise your follow-ups accordingly.
  3. Pattern recognition: If a specific division or person is consistently late, you can address this directly with the client by offering additional support or adjusting expectations for that area.

Additional Strategies for Difficult Content Collection

  • Offer to conduct interviews: If the client struggles to write content, offer to interview them (by phone or video call) and write the content yourself based on their answers. This is faster for them and gives you more control over quality. Factor the time into your project estimate.
  • Break large requests into smaller tasks: Instead of asking for "all 7 division overviews," ask for one division per week. Smaller asks feel more achievable.
  • Provide examples: Write one division overview as a sample and send it to the client as a reference for the remaining six. People write better when they have a model to follow.
  • Set up a content workshop: Block 2-3 hours with the client and key team members. Walk through the templates together, answer questions in real-time, and get as much content drafted as possible in one session.
  • Identify a content champion: Ask the client to designate one person whose job it is to coordinate content collection across all divisions. Having a single point of accountability is far more effective than chasing seven different people.
  • Send weekly status updates: Every Friday, send a brief email summarising what has been received, what is outstanding, and what is due the following week. This keeps the project visible and creates ongoing accountability without being pushy.
  • Celebrate submissions: When content arrives on time and is high quality, acknowledge it. A brief "Thank you, the Crop Farming overview is excellent and exactly what we needed" reinforces good behaviour and motivates the team to keep delivering.

Remember

Content collection difficulty is not a reflection of the client's commitment to the project. Most clients genuinely want to provide content but are overwhelmed by the volume, unsure of what to write, or simply too busy with their core business. Your role is to make it as easy as possible for them to succeed, while protecting yourself and the project from the consequences of delays.
