Section 03

What We Identified

During and after our conversation, I identified several critical areas that will shape how this project succeeds. These are the questions and considerations that, if left unaddressed, would compromise the quality of anything we build.

3.1   Audience: Who Is This Website For?

A website that tries to speak to everyone ends up speaking to no one. Your website will be visited by fundamentally different people with fundamentally different goals:

Audience What They Want What They Need to See
Investors and Partners Scale, credibility, leadership, financial stability Corporate overview, leadership team, track record, portfolio breadth
Customers (Agriculture) Products, pricing, reliability, delivery Product catalogues, farm operations, supply chain information
Customers (Real Estate) Properties, availability, trustworthiness Listings, project photos, location details, contact
Customers (Oil & Gas) Capabilities, contract history, certifications Service offerings, equipment, project portfolio
Government and Regulatory Bodies Compliance, certifications, legitimacy Licences, regulatory certifications (NAFDAC, NUPRC), governance
Job Seekers Culture, openings, growth opportunities Career page, team culture, open positions
General Public Understanding what the company does Clear, simple overview with compelling visuals
The Implication
The homepage cannot be optimised for all of these audiences at the same time. We will need to decide together who the primary audience is, and design the entry experience around that priority. This is a strategic business decision, not a design decision.

3.2   Scope: One Website or Seven?

This may be the single most important question of the entire project. Does your business operate under one unified brand, or do the individual divisions carry separate identities in the market?

The answer determines whether we are building:

Option Description Comparable Example
A One corporate group website with divisional sections Dangote Group (dangote.com)
B A family of related websites with a shared identity system Tata Group (tata.com)
C A corporate parent site plus standalone sites for select divisions Oando PLC (oandoplc.com)

Each option carries different implications for design complexity, development time, content requirements, and investment. We will resolve this together during the Discovery phase.

3.3   Content: The Silent Project Risk

In my experience with projects of this scale, the most common reason websites stall is not design disagreements or technical challenges. It is content.

Seven divisions could require:

  • 50 to 100 pages of written content, including descriptions, service pages, team profiles, and case studies
  • Professional photography across all operations, from farms and processing facilities to properties, petrol stations, and offices
  • Team biographies and leadership profiles
  • Certifications, licences, and compliance documentation
  • Success stories and project portfolios for each division
Critical Question
How much of this content exists today? Who will create what does not yet exist? The answers directly impact the timeline and investment, and they need to be addressed before design begins.

3.4   The "Test of Time" Question

You mentioned wanting a website that stands the test of time. I genuinely respect that aspiration, and I want to be transparent about what it actually requires.

A website is a living asset. Markets change, team members move on, new projects are completed, and new verticals may be added in the future. A website that never changes eventually looks abandoned, and an abandoned website signals an inactive business to anyone who visits.

What Stays Constant What Must Evolve
Brand identity and visual systemNews, updates, and announcements
Core site architecture and navigationTeam members and leadership
Design system and component libraryProject portfolios and case studies
Technology foundationProduct and service offerings
Security and performance infrastructureRegulatory and compliance information
Our Solution
The answer is not a website that never needs updating. The answer is a website built on a foundation so solid that updates are easy, fast, and do not require a developer. That is exactly what we will build together.

3.5   Understanding Your Business Layers

Not all seven divisions are at the same stage of maturity, and they should not all receive the same weight on the website. Presenting every division equally can actually weaken your strongest areas by diluting them alongside divisions that are earlier in their development.

During our Discovery Workshop, we will work together to understand which of your divisions fall into each of these three strategic layers:

Layer What It Means How the Website Treats It
Core Operations Divisions that are actively generating revenue, have real operational depth, and represent your strongest market position today These lead the website. They get the deepest pages, the strongest visuals, and the most prominent positioning. This is what visitors see first.
Supporting Capabilities Divisions that strengthen your overall offering and are operational, but may not need full standalone visibility yet These are presented as extensions of the core, showing breadth without competing for attention. Clear and professional, but not the headline.
Strategic Vision Divisions that represent future ambitions, planned expansions, or early-stage operations that are not yet fully established These are positioned as forward-looking direction. They communicate ambition and growth without making operational claims the business cannot yet support.

This layered approach means the website leads with your strongest foot forward, supports it with breadth, and signals ambition without overpromising. It also means the site can grow naturally as divisions mature from Vision into Supporting, and from Supporting into Core.

The Key Question

During our Discovery Workshop, I will ask you a simple but important question:

"If we could only showcase one part of your business online today, which one would it be?"

Your answer will guide how we structure everything. It reveals what matters most right now, and it ensures we build from a position of strength rather than spreading too thin.

What We Need to Clarify Together

Before we can structure the website effectively, we need honest answers to these questions for each of the seven divisions:

  • Is this division currently active and generating revenue, or is it planned for the future?
  • Does it have real assets, projects, or client relationships we can showcase?
  • Does it have an existing team, physical operations, or a documented track record?
  • If we removed it from the website entirely, would your business be misrepresented?

These are not judgement calls about which divisions matter. They are practical questions that ensure we present your business accurately and build something that genuinely serves it.

Section 05

Understanding Brand Architecture

A conglomerate with seven divisions faces a strategic question that most single-industry businesses never encounter: How should the divisions relate to each other visually?

There are four established models, and each one produces a very different result:

Model A: Branded House

Think Google: Maps, Drive, Cloud, all carry the Google name (about.google)

Every division carries your company name and visual identity. Maximum brand cohesion, where every division reinforces the parent.

Strongest group identity Divisions feel less independent

Model B: House of Brands

Think Unilever: Dove, Axe, Lipton, no visible connection (unilever.com)

Each division has its own independent name, logo, and identity. The parent company exists only at the corporate level.

Maximum market flexibility Most expensive to develop

Model C: Endorsed Brands

Think Marriott: Courtyard by Marriott, Ritz-Carlton (marriott.com)

Each division has its own personality but carries your company's endorsement. "Powered by" or "A [COMPANY NAME] Company."

Best of both worlds Requires careful visual balance

Model D: Hybrid

Custom approach, different strategies for different divisions

Some divisions operate under the parent brand while others carry more independent identities, based on the specific market they serve.

Maximum flexibility Requires the most strategic thought
What Happens Next
We will determine the right model for your business together during the Discovery phase. This is not a design decision. It is a business strategy decision that affects how your company is perceived in every market you operate in.

A Preview of What's Possible

To give you a sense of the creative direction we would explore, here are two concepts for the homepage experience:

Concept 1: Story-First Homepage

"From the Soil to the Shelf to the World"

Instead of presenting seven divisions as separate boxes on a page, we tell the story of your business as an integrated journey:

The Narrative Flow:
The land produces → We farm and raise livestock → We process the harvest → We market the commodities → We build the infrastructure → We trade globally → We power the economy

Each scroll section introduces a division as a chapter in a larger story. The visitor does not see seven unrelated businesses. They see one powerful conglomerate operating across the entire value chain.

Why this works: It turns your complexity from a navigation problem into your greatest selling point.

Concept 2: Audience-Guided Entry

"Welcome. I am..."

Instead of pushing every visitor through the same homepage experience, we greet them with a simple question and let them choose their own path:

An Investor looking to understand the portfolio → corporate overview, financials, leadership
A Customer looking for products or services → relevant divisional pages
A Partner exploring collaboration → capabilities, project history, contact
A Job Seeker looking for opportunities → careers, culture, openings

Each path pulls together content from across all seven divisions, tailored specifically to what that visitor cares about.

Why this works: A website that understands its visitor before they even scroll feels intelligent, considered, and genuinely professional.

These are directions, not final decisions. We will explore, refine, and test these concepts together during Stage 2.

Section 14

Why a Structured Approach Matters

I completely understand the desire to move quickly. Speed is valuable, and I respect it. But in projects of this scope, moving fast without a strategy tends to produce expensive problems down the line:

When Projects Skip... What Typically Happens
Audience analysisThe homepage ends up designed for the wrong people. A redesign becomes necessary.
Brand guidelinesThe visual identity feels inconsistent. Every page looks like it belongs to a different site.
Content strategyDesign is approved. Development is complete. Then launch stalls because nobody wrote the content.
Information architectureNavigation confuses visitors. Key pages get buried. Bounce rates climb.
Approval workflowConflicting feedback arrives from multiple stakeholders. An 8-week project quietly becomes a 20-week project.
Maintenance planThe site launches beautifully. A year later, outdated information turns it into a liability.
The Bottom Line
A structured approach does not slow the project down. It prevents the project from needing to be done twice. The fastest path to a world-class result is doing it right the first time.
