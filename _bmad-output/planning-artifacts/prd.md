# Product Requirements Document - UK_Web_Design

**Author:** Awwal
**Date:** 2026-03-24

## Executive Summary

This project will deliver a world-class multi-division corporate web platform for a client operating across crop farming, animal husbandry, agro-processing, commodity marketing, real estate, importing/exporting, and oil and gas. The product is not intended to function as a simple brochure website. It is intended to serve as the digital foundation of the business: a credible, extensible, mobile-first platform that presents the full scope of the company with clarity, authority, and long-term structural integrity.

The core business problem is not merely the absence of a website. The deeper problem is the absence of a coherent digital expression of professionalism, credibility, and commercial seriousness across a complex multi-vertical business. The platform must therefore do more than display information. It must convert business breadth into trust, make the company legible to external audiences, and position the client as an organisation that is serious, modern, and ready for partnerships, customers, and investment.

The product will be designed and built as a durable system rather than a one-time visual artifact. It must be clean, aesthetically strong, fast, accessible, SEO-ready, mobile-optimized, and extensible. It must also provide a stable architectural foundation that can absorb future business changes, content growth, and platform evolution without requiring a structural rebuild.

### What Makes This Special

What distinguishes this project is that the website itself is expected to function as a business credibility engine. Its value is not limited to online presence. It is meant to signal professionalism, operational maturity, and trustworthiness to investors, partners, customers, and the wider public. The product must therefore balance premium presentation with serious information architecture and technical discipline.

The core insight behind the project is that for a multi-division business, design quality alone is insufficient. The real differentiator is the ability to translate complexity into clarity. If the platform succeeds, users should not experience the business as a confusing collection of unrelated sectors. They should experience it as a coherent, high-quality organisation with scale, intent, and structure.

The product’s differentiator will come from the combination of strong visual execution, premium interaction quality, high-performance implementation, and a scalable content and component foundation. That combination is what turns the platform from “a website” into an asset the business can build on.

## Project Classification

- **Project Type:** Web application / corporate website platform

## Success Criteria

### User Success

The primary user success condition is frictionless navigation. Visitors should be able to arrive on the website, understand what the business does, identify the relevant division or pathway for their needs, and reach the next useful action without confusion or cognitive overload.

Because the platform serves multiple audience groups at launch, user success must work across investors and partners, customers, and the general public. The site should therefore make the company legible quickly, present the business structure clearly, and reduce the usual confusion that comes with multi-division corporate websites.

User success will be evident when:

- Visitors understand the company’s breadth and structure within the first visit
- Users can move from homepage to relevant division or contact path without getting lost
- The experience feels modern, trustworthy, and professionally executed
- The site performs well enough on mobile that the experience feels smooth rather than compromised

### Business Success

The first business objective is credibility. The platform must make the client appear serious, professional, and investment-worthy to external audiences. It should strengthen first impressions and improve the business’s perceived legitimacy and maturity.

The next business objectives are inquiries, partnerships, and visibility. The website should create a stronger platform for inbound contact, partner confidence, and public-facing brand presence. Recruitment is explicitly out of scope as a primary business goal for the initial release.

Business success will be evident when:

- The client is strongly satisfied with the outcome and sees it as a serious representation of the business
- The website improves trust during partner and investor conversations
- The business receives stronger or more qualified inquiries
- The company’s public image improves through a more professional digital presence
- Visitors engage with the site long enough to explore more than one section or division

### Technical Success

Technical success is defined primarily by speed, SEO readiness, maintainability, and accessibility. The website should not merely look premium; it should perform like a serious production system.

Technical success requires:

- Fast page loads, especially on mobile and constrained networks
- Strong SEO foundations across structure, metadata, semantics, and crawlability
- Maintainable code architecture that supports future iteration without structural rewrites
- Accessibility-conscious implementation so the experience remains usable across a broad audience
- A custom-coded implementation that preserves flexibility and engineering control, rather than relying on low-code or no-code constraints

### Measurable Outcomes

The project should be treated as successful when the following outcomes are achieved:

- The client explicitly approves the platform as a high-quality, world-class representation of the business
- Users can navigate from the homepage to key sections or divisions without confusion
- Core pages load quickly on mobile devices under realistic conditions
- The site launches with strong Lighthouse-oriented quality goals across performance, accessibility, SEO, and best practices
- The platform supports visibility and inquiry generation through clear contact paths and discoverable structure
- Post-launch engagement indicates that users are exploring the site rather than bouncing immediately

## User Journeys

### Journey 1: Investor / Partner Visitor - Credibility and Opportunity Path

An investor, institutional contact, or strategic partner lands on the website because they want to assess whether this business is serious, structured, and worth engaging. They arrive with skepticism and limited time. Their first need is not decoration but clarity.

The homepage must immediately communicate that this is a credible multi-division company, not a vague collection of ambitions. The visitor should quickly understand the company’s sectors, how the business is structured, and what makes it commercially meaningful. From there, they should be able to move into corporate overview content, divisional capability pages, news and thought leadership, and contact or partnership pathways without friction.

The key value moment occurs when the visitor shifts from “what exactly is this company?” to “this is a serious organisation with enough structure and professionalism to justify a conversation.” The journey succeeds when the site supports trust, partner confidence, and follow-up engagement.

This journey reveals requirements for:

- Strong homepage narrative and hierarchy
- Clear corporate overview and group structure
- Divisional capability presentation
- Optional audience-based entry points that accelerate orientation for investor and partner visitors
- General company news and thought leadership stream
- Prominent partnership or inquiry pathways
- Premium visual and technical execution to reinforce trust

### Journey 2: Customer / Prospect Visitor - Division Discovery and Inquiry Path

A prospect arrives looking for a product, service, capability, or business unit relevant to their need. They may not know the corporate structure in advance. Their success depends on finding the correct division quickly and understanding whether the business can serve them.

The journey begins at the homepage, search/navigation, or a division landing page. The prospect should be able to identify the relevant vertical without stress, move into that section, understand the offer, and reach a division-specific inquiry path. Each division page should therefore be more than a placeholder. It should communicate competence, relevance, and a clear next step.

The key value moment occurs when the visitor no longer feels like they are browsing a generic corporate site, but instead feels they have reached the right business unit with a clear way to engage. The journey succeeds when the visitor can move from discovery to inquiry with confidence.

This journey reveals requirements for:

- Clear division navigation and grouping
- Distinct division overview pages
- Division-specific service or capability framing
- Division-specific inquiry flows
- Strong contact architecture and conversion pathways
- Content depth sufficient to support confidence before inquiry

### Journey 3: General Public Visitor - Understanding and Legitimacy Path

A general public visitor lands on the site to understand what the company does. They may have heard the name, seen a reference, or encountered the brand indirectly. Their main question is simple: is this a real, serious, professionally run business?

The journey begins with the need for orientation. The website must explain the company clearly, show the relationship between the divisions, and communicate legitimacy without forcing the visitor to decode the structure themselves. The user should leave with an accurate sense of the company’s breadth, professionalism, and operational seriousness.

The key value moment occurs when the visitor can summarise the company in plain language after a short visit. The journey succeeds when confusion is replaced by clarity and legitimacy.

This journey reveals requirements for:

- Strong group narrative on the homepage
- Clear explanation of business structure
- Accessible language and visual hierarchy
- A deliberate balance between corporate authority and human approachability in tone and presentation
- Professional presentation that supports public trust
- Supporting pages that explain the company without overload

### Journey 4: Internal Content Owner - Controlled Update and Growth Path

An internal content owner needs to keep the platform alive over time. Even in a custom-coded system, the business must have a practical way to publish news, thought leadership, and selected content updates without turning every change into a redevelopment task.

The journey begins when the business wants to publish a company-wide update or a division-specific insight. The content owner should be able to create, edit, organize, and publish content in a controlled way that preserves structure and quality. They should also be able to place content in the correct stream: general company or division-specific.

The key value moment occurs when the business realises the platform is not frozen after launch. It is stable, but still usable as a living communication asset. The journey succeeds when content freshness can be maintained without compromising platform quality.

This journey reveals requirements for:

- Content management path for general news and division-specific publishing
- Structured publishing model and taxonomy
- Controlled editing workflow for maintainability
- Reusable page and content templates
- Architecture that supports ongoing updates without structural drift

### Journey Requirements Summary

The journeys above reveal the need for the following capability areas:

- Homepage narrative that explains the business clearly and professionally
- Corporate overview and group structure pages
- Division-level information architecture and presentation pages
- Division-specific inquiry flows for conversion and professionalism
- General company news and thought leadership stream
- Division-specific news and thought leadership streams
- Clear contact and partnership pathways

## Domain-Specific Requirements

### Compliance & Regulatory

The platform is not itself a regulated operational system, but it represents business units that may operate under sector-specific compliance regimes. The website should therefore be designed to support credibility and disclosure without making unverifiable claims.

Requirements:

- The platform must support structured presentation of certifications, licenses, operational capabilities, and compliance-related business information where available.
- Division pages must be written and designed to avoid implying regulatory approval, operational guarantees, or legal claims that the client cannot substantiate.
- The system should allow future inclusion of compliance-focused content areas for relevant divisions such as oil and gas, agro-processing, and import/export.
- Content governance should assume that some divisions may require more careful review before publication.

### Technical Constraints

Because the platform is a public-facing credibility and inquiry system, the main domain-specific technical constraints are trust, clarity, performance, and maintainability rather than transaction security or regulated workflow handling.

Requirements:

- The site must load quickly on mobile and variable network conditions.
- The architecture must support multi-division content without collapsing into confusing navigation.
- The implementation must preserve high-quality SEO semantics across both general company pages and division-specific pages.
- Accessibility must be treated as a baseline quality requirement because the platform serves a broad public and commercial audience.
- The platform must be maintainable enough to support future expansion of content, divisions, and publishing streams.

### Integration Requirements

No hard third-party integration set has been confirmed yet, but the platform should be prepared for structured future integrations.

Requirements:

- Contact and inquiry flows should support division-specific routing logic.
- Publishing architecture should support both company-wide and division-specific news/thought leadership streams.
- The codebase should be structured so future integrations such as analytics, CRM capture, search, media libraries, or structured content backends can be added without redesigning the front end.
- The content model should anticipate future additions such as certifications, project showcases, media galleries, and partner-facing materials.

### Risk Mitigations

The main domain risks are not software-regulation failure, but misrepresentation, confusion, and structural inconsistency.

Mitigations:

- Use clear division architecture so users do not mistake the business for an incoherent collection of unrelated claims.
- Keep copy and visual presentation precise enough to signal professionalism without exaggeration.
- Design the system to scale by pattern rather than by one-off pages.
- Support content freshness through a publishing path for news and thought leadership so the website does not become stale and credibility-damaging.
- Keep future compliance-oriented content as a supported capability rather than hardcoding assumptions too early.

## Web Application Specific Requirements

### Project-Type Overview

This product should be implemented as a web-first corporate platform rather than an app-like single-page application. The experience must prioritize clarity, performance, SEO, accessibility, and long-term maintainability over client-side complexity. The correct delivery model is an MPA-first architecture with strong page-level semantics, robust routing, and progressive enhancement where needed.

This choice aligns with the project’s primary goals: premium presentation, discoverability, fast loading, multi-division information architecture, and a durable technical foundation for future extension.

### Technical Architecture Considerations

The platform should be structured as a content-rich, SEO-sensitive web application with clear server-rendered or statically generated page boundaries. The implementation should support strong metadata control, semantic HTML, predictable routing, and efficient asset delivery.

Key technical considerations:

- MPA-first architecture for strong SEO, fast initial loads, and clean content separation
- Modern browser baseline with graceful behavior across current evergreen browsers
- SEO treated as a first-class architecture concern from day one, not a post-build patch
- No real-time features required for MVP
- Accessibility aligned to WCAG 2.1 as an explicit quality target
- Mobile-first responsive behavior across layout, media, navigation, and interaction patterns
- Component-based front-end structure that supports reuse across company and division pages
- Structured content patterns that allow general and division-specific publishing streams

### Browser Matrix

The platform should support current versions of major evergreen browsers used by modern desktop and mobile audiences.

Requirements:

- Support current stable versions of Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari
- Support modern Android and iOS browsers for mobile-first use cases
- Avoid reliance on fragile or experimental browser APIs for core functionality
- Ensure graceful degradation where minor visual effects are not supported

### Responsive Design

Responsive behavior is a core requirement, not an enhancement. The platform must be designed for mobile use first and then scaled upward for tablet and desktop layouts.

Requirements:

- Mobile-first layout and interaction design
- Navigation patterns optimized for small screens
- Readable typography and touch-friendly targets across breakpoints
- Media and content blocks that adapt cleanly across device classes
- Section and page templates that remain coherent across varied content lengths

### Performance Targets

Performance is a credibility feature for this project. Slow loading undermines professionalism and usability.

Requirements:

- Fast initial page render on mobile devices and constrained networks
- Optimized asset delivery, image strategy, and font loading
- Lightweight front-end behavior with restrained client-side scripting
- Strong Lighthouse-oriented performance targets for production pages
- Avoid animation or interaction patterns that materially degrade responsiveness

### SEO Strategy

SEO must be built into the page architecture, content structure, and metadata model from the outset.

Requirements:

- Semantic heading hierarchy and crawlable page structure
- Clean URLs and page-level metadata support
- Support for division pages, company pages, and publishing content with strong discoverability
- Technical SEO readiness including sitemap, robots strategy, canonical handling where needed, and structured metadata support
- Internal linking patterns that reinforce both corporate and division discoverability

### Accessibility Level

Accessibility should be implemented against WCAG 2.1 expectations as part of normal delivery quality.

Requirements:

- Semantic markup and accessible navigation structure
- Keyboard-usable interactive components
- Adequate contrast and readable typography
- Meaningful focus states and form labeling
- Motion restraint and interaction behavior that does not impair usability
- Accessibility considered during component design, not retrofitted after implementation

### Implementation Considerations

The implementation should preserve engineering flexibility while still supporting ongoing publishing and structured content updates.

Requirements:

- Architecture should support both general company and division-specific publishing streams
- Inquiry pathways should support division-specific routing
- The codebase should be organized to allow future growth without major rewrites
- Content-heavy pages must be built from reusable section patterns rather than one-off layouts
- Technical decisions should favor maintainability and extensibility over unnecessary complexity

## Project Scoping & Phased Development

### MVP Strategy & Philosophy

**MVP Approach:** Foundation-quality MVP

This MVP is not intended to test whether the business should have a digital presence. That need is already established. The purpose of the MVP is to launch a complete, credible, extensible first release that already performs the website’s core business job: communicating professionalism, trust, and multi-division clarity.

The MVP must therefore solve the full credibility problem from day one, while limiting expansion in depth rather than compromising the quality of the foundation.

**Resource Requirements:**
At minimum, this requires strong product direction, UX/design discipline, front-end engineering, content structuring, and deployment/QA coverage. Even if executed by one lead builder, the work must be handled as a multi-disciplinary production effort rather than a quick design-to-code exercise.

### MVP Feature Set (Phase 1)

**Core User Journeys Supported:**

- Investor/partner credibility and opportunity journey
- Customer/prospect division discovery and inquiry journey
- General public understanding and legitimacy journey
- Internal content owner publishing journey for company-wide and division-specific updates

**Must-Have Capabilities:**

- Strong homepage narrative and corporate positioning
- Corporate overview and business structure pages
- Division overview and individual division presentation pages
- Clear mobile-first navigation across the full business structure
- Site search for cross-platform content discovery
- Division-specific inquiry flows
- General company news and thought leadership stream
- Division-specific news and thought leadership streams
- Strong SEO architecture and metadata support
- WCAG 2.1-conscious accessibility implementation
- Fast, production-grade performance on mobile and modern browsers
- Reusable component and page-section architecture for future growth
- Maintainable custom-coded implementation with room for future structured content expansion

### Post-MVP Features

**Phase 2 (Post-MVP):**

- Deeper content expansion per division
- Case studies, project stories, and media galleries
- Expanded partner/investor-oriented content
- Richer content taxonomy and archive filtering
- Enhanced analytics and inquiry tracking, including division-aware measurement where useful
- More advanced editorial workflows if needed

**Phase 3 (Expansion):**

- Additional division-specific subsections and deeper service architecture
- More advanced search, filtering, and content discovery features
- Extended integration layer for CRM, media systems, or structured back-office content tools
- New business-unit launches using the established design and component system
- Further reputation-building content ecosystems across multiple audience types

### Risk Mitigation Strategy

**Technical Risks:**
The biggest technical risk is letting a content-heavy multi-division platform become structurally inconsistent. Mitigation: use reusable page patterns, disciplined information architecture, and a stable component system from the outset.

**Market Risks:**
The main market risk is not lack of demand, but failure to meet the quality bar implied by the client’s expectations. Mitigation: prioritize visible quality, clarity, and credibility in the MVP rather than spreading effort thin across excessive feature depth.

**Resource Risks:**
The main resource risk is scope expansion through division-by-division overbuilding. Mitigation: keep all major platform capabilities in MVP, but limit initial depth where necessary. Build one strong system, not seven disconnected websites.
