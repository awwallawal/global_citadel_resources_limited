## Session Overview

**Topic:** Structuring a professional web engagement for a multi-vertical conglomerate — from post-discovery documentation through phased delivery
**Goals:** (a) Create the post-call alignment document, (b) Define engagement phases, (c) Identify hard questions before design begins, (d) Position as strategic partner, not vendor

### Context Guidance

_Client runs a diversified conglomerate spanning 7 verticals: Crop Farming, Animal Husbandry, Agro-Processing, Commodity Marketing, Real Estate, Import/Export, Oil & Gas. Client expects fast turnaround. Engagement must slow things down professionally while building confidence._

### Session Setup

_Client provided business portfolio (call.txt), not a website brief. Core challenge reframed from "how to structure a web design process" to "how to translate a multi-sector conglomerate's business portfolio into a coherent digital presence with a professional engagement process that educates the client on what's actually required."_

### Key Reframes Established Early

- "We need a website" is not a business objective — it's a wish
- The call.txt file is a business portfolio, not a website brief
- Design prototypes are NOT the next step — discovery and branding come first
- "Prove myself" is the wrong frame — "establish a partnership" is correct
- A website is a living document that needs maintenance — client was educated on this
- Branding must precede web design — you don't design a house before choosing the bricks

## Technique Execution Results

### Phase 1: Question Storming

**Interactive Focus:** Generating the complete universe of unknowns before proposing solutions. User contributed raw, unfiltered thinking ("word vomit") which was converted into structured questions.

**Key Breakthroughs:**

- The discovery that branding must precede website design — and that this is potentially two projects, not one
- The realization that "prove myself" is solved by the alignment document itself, not the final website
- The client's compliment (structuring his thinking into 7 themes) is already proof of competence
- Brand architecture models (Branded House vs. House of Brands) as a strategic decision most web designers never raise

**User Creative Strengths:** Raw, honest self-reflection. Willingness to expose fears and uncertainties. Natural strategic instinct buried under self-doubt.

**Energy Level:** High engagement throughout — user's "word vomit" style produced rich, unfiltered material that the facilitator could refine.

### Phase 2: Six Thinking Hats × Deep Domain Mining

**Building on Previous:** Questions from Phase 1 were systematically examined through six perspectives, applied to previously untouched domains.

**New Insights by Hat:**

- **White Hat (Facts):** Exposed critical unknowns — user journeys unmapped, SEO strategy undefined for 7 industries, regulatory/compliance requirements per vertical unexamined
- **Red Hat (Emotions):** Identified the core emotional design tension — corporate authority vs. human approachability. Named the client's likely emotional response to seeing real scope.
- **Yellow Hat (Opportunities):** This project is a portfolio centrepiece, each vertical is a potential long-term client relationship, Nigerian conglomerate market is digitally underserved
- **Black Hat (Risks):** Content collection silence as #1 project killer, scope creep via "just one more thing," client taking Phase 1 deliverables to cheaper developer
- **Green Hat (Creative):** Story-first homepage concept, audience-based entry points, the alignment document as a designed portfolio piece, paid discovery sprint model
- **Blue Hat (Process):** 8 phases condensed to 3 client-facing stages, approval workflow with deadlines, client responsibilities on the timeline

**Developed Ideas:** Two breakthrough UX concepts — story-first homepage narrative ("From soil to shelf to world") and audience-based entry points ("I am an [Investor/Customer/Partner]")

## Session Summary and Insights

### Key Achievements

- **100 ideas generated** across 7 thematic areas using Question Storming and Six Thinking Hats (fused with deep domain mining)
- **Core challenge reframed** from "how to structure a web design process" to "how to translate a multi-sector business portfolio into a coherent digital presence with a professional engagement process"
- **3 breakthrough concepts** identified: story-first homepage narrative, audience-based entry points, design system approach
- **Professional positioning discovered:** "I bring clarity to complex businesses" — derived from client's own validation
- **Complete alignment document structure** mapped section-by-section with emotional arc design
- **7-day action plan** created with specific daily outputs

### Creative Breakthroughs

1. **Story-first homepage (#69):** "From soil to shelf to world" — transforms 7 competing verticals from an information architecture problem into a compelling narrative. The conglomerate's diversity becomes a story of integration, not a grid of boxes.
2. **Audience-based entry points (#70):** "I am an [Investor/Customer/Partner/Job Seeker]" — inverts site architecture from business-centric to user-centric. Solves the "website serves everyone" problem elegantly.
3. **Design system approach (#81):** Delivering a component library rather than a frozen website. New verticals, new pages assembled from existing components. The technical answer to "stands the test of time."

### Session Reflections

**User Creative Strengths:** Awwal's raw, unfiltered "word vomit" style produced extraordinarily rich material. His willingness to expose fears, uncertainties, and half-formed thoughts allowed the facilitator to extract, sharpen, and structure ideas that were already present in his thinking but hadn't been articulated. His natural instinct is to bring clarity to chaos — which, notably, is exactly what the client praised him for.

**Critical Mindset Applied:** Per user's directive ("Critique, Criticise, Clarify — Don't Validate Assumptions"), every assumption was challenged: that design prototypes are the next step (they aren't — discovery is), that the client's compliment needs supplementing with more proof (it doesn't — the alignment document IS the proof), that "proving himself" is the right frame (it isn't — "establishing a partnership" is), and that doing everything personally demonstrates competence (it doesn't — it risks burnout and underpricing).

**Key Insight:** The fear wasn't incompetence or scope — it was wasted effort on a client who might not value professional work. The alignment document solves this: it's simultaneously a deliverable for the client AND a qualification filter. A client who reads it and says "this is too much" has self-selected out. A client who reads it and says "when do we start" has confirmed alignment. Either outcome is a win.
